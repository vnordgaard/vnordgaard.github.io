Publications

Differential treatment close to the election?

Victor Nordgaard (published as Hejstvig-Larsen) & Ebbe Kristiansen, 2024, politica

This article investigates whether the electoral cycle – the phenomenon where politicians formulate more generous policy close to elections – is moderated by the deservingness status of the recipient of said generous policy. We argue that strategic politicians will design generous policy only for deserving recipients whose welfare needs are perceived as self-inflicted. To test this, we coded all adopted acts and parliamentary resolutions in the Danish parliament from 1975–2019 that target five societal groups. With linear probability models, we find that politicians do in fact introduce more generous policies for deserving groups close to elections, while the effect of the electoral cycle is non-existent for undeserving groups. Our findings point towards a more disaggregated approach in the study of the electoral cycle.

Reclaiming the City: How Citizens Mobilize When Their Preferences Are Visibly Discounted

Victor Nordgaard

How do citizens respond when politicians openly privilege an economic elite? Research on unequal representation shows a policy bias toward affluent preferences, yet the issues studied typically exhibit striking preference similarity across income groups. A complementary literature explains this low conflict as strategic: elites anticipate backlash and channel inequality into technical, low-salience domains to keep the public disengaged. This paper examines what happens when these strategies fail — when elites grant visible concessions that contradict majority preferences. Using surveys of citizens and businesses (n ≈ 5,500), behavioral data (inquiries and petitions), and elite interviews, the study analyzes a case in a Danish municipality where institutional arrangements enabled such an open concession. Findings show that visible privilege triggered conflict expansion: disengaged citizens mobilized, opinions crystallized, and issue salience spiked. The implication is that citizens — even when initially politically disengaged — can rise to hold decision-makers who openly favor the affluent accountable.

Does Affluence Shape Local Representation? The Relationship between Income and the Distribution of NIMBY goods

Paula Natalie Bings & Victor Nordgaard

Whether policymakers are equally responsive to all citizens is a principal question in democratic representation. While a sizable body of literature points to an affluent bias in policy representation, the evidence remains contested: income groups rarely diverge in stated preferences, estimates are sensitive to model specifications, and recent work finds no evidence of bias in government transfer allocations. A core difficulty is that existing studies infer representation from the correspondence between preferences and outcomes, which is complicated by the fact that preferences are highly correlated across income groups, differ in how informed they are, and observed outcomes fail to capture non-decisions.

We argue that the geographic placement of facilities and constructions that are collectively beneficial but locally undesirable (NIMBY-goods) offers a uniquely clean test of representation. Because no community wants such facilities nearby, their placement necessarily imposes costs on someone. Where those costs fall reveals whose interests policymakers prioritize. This sidesteps the problems with preference-based analysis and speaks directly to an unresolved question with fresh evidence.

We test this argument using facility placement across Danish municipalities in three steps. The first analysis asks the basic question: are locally undesirable facilities systematically more likely to be placed near lower-income areas? If so, that is a substantive finding in its own right: these neighborhoods absorb the burdens of collective provision. We then employ two designs targeting selection bias: a border-discontinuity approach comparing neighboring districts across municipal boundaries, and an exploitation of the 2007 municipal reform, which mechanically shifted districts' relative income ranks within newly merged jurisdictions. The results advance our understanding of income, public service delivery, and democratic representation.
