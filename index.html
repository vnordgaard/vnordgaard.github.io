About

I am a Ph.D. student at the Department of Political Science at Aarhus University. My research interests include representation, political behavior, and inequality. The focus of my dissertation is unequal representation, and it combines research on public opinion, elite perceptions, and political economy.

I hold a BA and an MA in Political Science from Aarhus University.

Publications

Differential treatment close to the election?

Victor Nordgaard (published as Hejstvig-Larsen) & Ebbe Kristiansen, 2024, politica

This article investigates whether the electoral cycle – the phenomenon where politicians formulate more generous policy close to elections – is moderated by the deservingness status of the recipient of said generous policy. We argue that strategic politicians will design generous policy only for deserving recipients whose welfare needs are perceived as self-inflicted. To test this, we coded all adopted acts and parliamentary resolutions in the Danish parliament from 1975–2019 that target five societal groups. With linear probability models, we find that politicians do in fact introduce more generous policies for deserving groups close to elections, while the effect of the electoral cycle is non-existent for undeserving groups. Our findings point towards a more disaggregated approach in the study of the electoral cycle.

The Anticipatory Filter and the Measurement of Unequal Representation

Victor Nordgaard

Studies of unequal representation rely on policy questions that display remarkably little disagreement across income groups. This paper argues that this pattern should not be interpreted as evidence that income weakly explains political preferences. Instead, the observed issue space is shaped by an anticipatory filter that excludes policy proposals likely to generate visible redistributive conflict. As a result, survey-based studies estimate representation on a systematically biased set of issues. Combining text analysis of policy items with comparisons to a broader universe of policy proposals, the paper shows that surveyed issues likely understate the extent of latent class conflict and, by extension, the potential for unequal representation when measured through the relatively inconspicuous issues that make it to the agenda of survey organizations.

Different Fights: How Political Conflict Shapes What a Policy Is About

Victor Nordgaard

How can the act of making a political decision transform what that decision is about? When governing bodies decide, they do not only resolve a pre-existing dispute but can redefine it. Groups that played no part in the preceding conflict suddenly find themselves affected, and they may understand what is at stake in different terms.

This paper connects that puzzle to debates about democratic representation. Research on responsiveness documents how policy tracks citizen preferences; policy feedback research shows how policies can shape subsequent political demands. Constructivist theories of representation push this further, arguing that political representation does not merely reflect pre-existing interests but actively constitutes the groups, identities, and issues that feed into politics. Building on these traditions, I argue that a political decision does not simply resolve a dispute but can remake what the dispute is about.

The argument is developed through a close empirical analysis of a single case of local traffic restrictions. It combines behavioral mobilization data, a large-scale citizen survey (N = 4,014), elite interviews, and semantic analysis of open-ended responses. Opponents and supporters were not divided by opposing preferences toward the same decision — they understood it in fundamentally different terms. For opponents, the issue was mobility and accessibility. For supporters, it was urban quality and climate. The same decision, contested on entirely different grounds. This shows that the meaning of the policy object of democratic responsiveness is not given in advance but actively remade in the contestation that follows policy implementation.

Does Affluence Shape Local Representation? The Relationship between Income and the Distribution of NIMBY goods

Paula Natalie Bings & Victor Nordgaard

Whether policymakers are equally responsive to all citizens is a principal question in democratic representation. While a sizable body of literature points to an affluent bias in policy representation, the evidence remains contested: income groups rarely diverge in stated preferences, estimates are sensitive to model specifications, and recent work finds no evidence of bias in government transfer allocations. A core difficulty is that existing studies infer representation from the correspondence between preferences and outcomes, which is complicated by the fact that preferences are highly correlated across income groups, differ in how informed they are, and observed outcomes fail to capture non-decisions.

We argue that the geographic placement of facilities and constructions that are collectively beneficial but locally undesirable (NIMBY-goods) offers a uniquely clean test of representation. Where those costs fall reveals whose interests policymakers prioritize.

We test this argument using facility placement across Danish municipalities in three steps. The first analysis asks the basic question: are locally undesirable facilities systematically more likely to be placed near lower-income areas? We then employ two designs targeting selection bias: a border-discontinuity approach comparing neighboring districts across municipal boundaries, and an exploitation of the 2007 municipal reform, which mechanically shifted districts' relative income ranks within newly merged jurisdictions. The results advance our understanding of income, public service delivery, and democratic representation.
